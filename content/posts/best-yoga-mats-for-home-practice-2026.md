Two years ago I would have laughed at anyone spending $90 on a yoga mat. Seriously. My girlfriend at the time dragged me to a Saturday morning yoga class — one of those "just try it once" situations — and I grabbed a $20 BalanceFrom mat off Amazon the night before. Prime delivery. Done. Why would anyone spend more than that on a rectangle of rubber you sweat on?

Fast forward to now and I own three yoga mats. The $20 one lives in my garage for stretching after runs. And my daily mat is the [JadeYoga Harmony](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B000ECD6N2?tag=amazonfi08e0c-20) which costs $90, and honestly I'd buy it again tomorrow if mine got stolen.

But getting here took a wierd journey through five different mats. So let me save you the trouble.

The short version: if you do yoga at home more than twice a week, get the Jade Harmony. If you're just starting out or you do yoga like once a month, the BalanceFrom at $20 is genuinely fine. And the Manduka PRO at $140 is an amazing mat that most people don't actually need.

![JadeYoga Harmony yoga mat](/images/products/jade-harmony-yoga-mat-2.jpg)

I started yoga in early 2024. Just basic stuff — YouTube videos in my living room, maybe 20 minutes a few mornings a week. The BalanceFrom mat was perfectly adequate for this. It's a quarter inch thick, comes with a little carrying strap, and the grip is... acceptable. For $20 you're not getting premium anything but you're getting a functional surface that beats doing downward dog on carpet.

The problem showed up around month three. I was getting more into it — longer sessions, more challenging poses, sweating more. And the BalanceFrom gets *slippery* when wet. Like, dangerously slippery. I'm in warrior III trying to balance on one foot and my standing foot is slowly hydroplaning across the mat. Not ideal.

I looked into it and apparently this is a PVC mat thing. The material itself doesn't absorb moisture at all so sweat just sits on top and turns your mat into a slip-n-slide. A guy on the Outdoor Gear Lab review team mentioned the same issue — PVC mats are fine for gentle yoga but fall apart performance-wise once you start sweating.

So I went to Target and bought a Gaiam mat for about $25. Different brand, same problem. Slightly better texture on the surface but still PVC, still gets slippery, still smells like a pool float for the first week. I used it for maybe a month before getting frustrated.

Then I did what I always do when I'm annoyed — fell down a research rabbit hole. Wirecutter recommends the Jade Harmony. Garage Gym Reviews says Manduka PRO. Forbes likes the Lululemon mat. YouTube yoga people seem split between Jade and Manduka. Everyone has an opinion, nobody agrees.

I ended up ordering both the Jade Harmony and the Manduka PRO in the same week because I have no self control and Amazon makes returns easy. Here's what happened.

![Manduka PRO yoga mat](/images/products/manduka-pro-yoga-mat-1.jpg)

The Manduka PRO arrived first. $138 for a yoga mat. It felt absurd taking it out of the box. But I'll say this — the thing feels *substantial*. It's heavy. Like 7.5 pounds heavy. The material is dense and the construction screams quality. Manduka offers a lifetime guarantee they describe as "approximately 10 years" which is a funny way to define lifetime but whatever.

Here's the catch though. And nobody warned me about this clearly enough. The Manduka PRO needs a break-in period. Out of the box, the surface is almost slick. I did my first session on it and was sliding around worse than the $20 mat. I actually thought it was defective.

Turns out you need to practice on it for a few weeks — some people say sprinkle coarse sea salt on it and let it sit overnight. I tried that. It helped a little. After about 3 weeks of daily use it started gripping properly. But three weeks of breaking in a $140 product? That's a tough sell.

The Jade Harmony was different from the second I unrolled it. Natural rubber. You can feel it immediately — the surface has this organic tackiness that just grabs your hands and feet. No break-in needed. First session and I'm doing poses I'd been struggling with purely because the grip was there.

It does smell. I won't lie about that. Natural rubber has a rubbery smell that's pretty strong the first few days. I left mine unrolled in the garage for 48 hours with the windows open and it was mostly gone after that. Some people on Amazon reviews complain about the smell lasting weeks but I think those folks might just have sensitive noses. Mine was fine after a few days.

The grip when sweaty is where the Jade absolutely destroys everything else I've tried. It actually gets *grippier* when wet. This makes zero sense to me from a physics standpoint but it works. Hot yoga people on YouTube swear by Jade mats for this exact reason. Nicole from Garage Gym Reviews rated the Manduka's grip highly too, but from my testing the Jade was noticeably stickier.

After a month of switching between them I kept reaching for the Jade. The Manduka went back to Amazon.

Now, the Jade isn't perfect. Its thinner than the Manduka — about 3/16 of an inch versus 6mm. If you have sensitive knees you'll feel the floor more during kneeling poses. I fold mine over for stuff like camel pose and it works fine, but if joint cushioning is your priority the Manduka or even a thick PVC mat might be better.

It also wears faster than the Manduka. The Manduka PRO is basically indestructable — closed cell PVC that'll outlive you. The Jade is open-cell natural rubber and after about 18 months of near-daily use mine is showing wear where I put my feet in downward dog. Little bits of rubber flaking off. Jade says this is normal and it doesn't affect performance, which tracks with my experience. It still grips great. It just looks a bit rough.

![BalanceFrom GoYoga mat](/images/products/balancefrom-goyoga-mat-1.jpg)

Let me be real about the [BalanceFrom GoYoga](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B08KZ6MDTV?tag=amazonfi08e0c-20) because I think it gets either too much love or too much hate online. At $20, its the bestselling yoga mat on Amazon with like 100,000+ ratings. And for what it is? It's solid. The thickness is nice — a full quarter inch — and if you're doing gentle yoga, stretching, or bodyweight exercises on it, you really don't need more.

My mom uses one. She does 15 minutes of morning stretching and some light yoga poses. She's had hers for over a year and it works fine for her. Not everyone needs a performance mat. If you told me you do yoga casually a few times a month, I'd say save the $70 and grab this.

But if you practice regularly and you sweat even a little, upgrade. The grip difference between the BalanceFrom and the Jade Harmony is like the difference between driving on ice and driving on dry pavement. Dramatic and immediately obvious.

The [Manduka PRO](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B0D2FP6BXW?tag=amazonfi08e0c-20) I'd recommend to a very specific person — someone who wants one mat for the rest of their life, doesn't mind the break-in period, and values cushioning over grip. Yoga teachers who are on their mat for hours a day often prefer the Manduka because the density and thickness reduces joint fatigue over long sessions. If that's you, its worth the $140.

For everyone else — the [Jade Harmony](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B000ECD6N2?tag=amazonfi08e0c-20) at $90. It's what Wirecutter recommends, it's what most yoga practitioners I've talked to prefer, and after two years of using mine almost every day, I still think it's the best balance of grip, comfort, and price. Not the cheapest. Not the fanciest. Just the one that works the best for actual yoga.

One last thing — whatever mat you buy, get a 68 inch or longer. I'm 5'10" and the standard 68 inch Jade is just barely long enough. If you're taller, Jade makes a 74 inch version for about $10 more. Worth it. Nothing worse than your feet hanging off the edge during savasana. That's supposed to be the relaxing part.
